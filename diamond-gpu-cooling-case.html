01 — THE THERMAL WALL

HEAT IS KILLING
YOUR COMPUTE

Modern datacenter GPUs — H100 SXM, H200, B200 — operate with Thermal Design Powers exceeding 700W. At these power densities, thermal management is no longer a secondary concern. It is the primary constraint on sustained performance.

Modern GPUs implement DVFS — Dynamic Voltage and Frequency Scaling. When die temperature approaches the thermal junction limit (Tj,max ≈ 83–95°C), the GPU autonomously reduces clock speed and voltage to shed heat. This is throttling. Every millisecond spent throttling is a millisecond of degraded performance.

The conventional thermal stack — silicon die → TIM1 (thermal interface material) → copper IHS (Integrated Heat Spreader) → TIM2 → cooler — accumulates resistances at each interface. The weakest link is TIM1: standard indium or polymer-based TIM1 layers achieve only 3–10 W/m·K conductivity.

Diamond's thermal conductivity is 2,000–2,200 W/m·K. The opportunity is structural. The problem is solved with physics, not firmware.

02 — MATERIAL PHYSICS

WHY DIAMOND WINS.
ON EVERY AXIS.

🔷
Thermal Conductivity
q = −κ · ∇T

Diamond's thermal conductivity (κ) of 2,000–2,200 W/m·K exceeds copper (385 W/m·K) by 5.7× and silicon (150 W/m·K) by 14×. Heat flux per unit temperature gradient is simply unmatched. A thinner diamond interface layer conducts more heat than a much thicker copper spreader — with lower ΔT.

⚡
Electrical Isolation
ρ ≈ 10¹² Ω·cm

Unlike copper, CVD diamond is an electrical insulator with resistivity >10¹² Ω·cm. This eliminates short-circuit risk when placing diamond directly at die-level interfaces without additional dielectric layers — simplifying the thermal stack and reducing interface count, which directly reduces cumulative resistance.

🧊
Coefficient of Thermal Expansion
α ≈ 1.0 ppm/°C

Diamond's CTE of ~1.0 ppm/°C closely matches silicon (~2.6 ppm/°C) and GaN. This near-match dramatically reduces thermo-mechanical stress at the die interface during thermal cycling — preventing delamination, micro-cracking, and long-term reliability degradation that plagues metal spreaders.

03 — RETROFIT SOLUTION ARCHITECTURE

THREE-PHASE
DIAMOND STACK

1
Phase 01 — Immediate Deploy
Diamond TIM1 Replacement

The GPU package lid is removed (delidding). The factory TIM1 — typically a low-conductivity indium solder or polymer pad — is replaced with a nano-diamond-loaded TIM or a brazed CVD diamond film, bonded directly between the bare die and the IHS.

◇ κ improvement: 3 W/m·K → 800+ W/m·K (nano-diamond composite)
◇ ΔT reduction: ~4–6°C at junction
◇ Process: Delidding + precision bond
◇ Reversibility: Full — lid re-sealed
2
Phase 02 — High Performance
CVD Diamond IHS Swap

The OEM copper IHS (Integrated Heat Spreader) is replaced with a precision-machined CVD diamond wafer plate. Diamond's lateral spreading resistance is 5× lower than copper, dramatically reducing hotspot ΔT across the die footprint before heat reaches the cooler.

◇ Spreader κ: 385 W/m·K (Cu) → 2000 W/m·K (CVD)
◇ ΔT reduction: additional 3–5°C
◇ Thickness: 0.5–1.5mm CVD diamond wafer
◇ Format: Single-crystal or polycrystalline CVD
3
Phase 03 — Full Stack
Diamond-to-Coldplate Bonding

A precision diamond-copper composite interface bridges the CVD IHS to the liquid cold plate. Diamond micropillar arrays or diamond-coated vapor chambers eliminate TIM2 resistance entirely via direct metallic bonding, achieving the minimum possible junction-to-coolant resistance.

◇ TIM2 elimination: replaces thermal paste (1–3 W/m·K)
◇ Interface: Transient liquid phase bonding or silver sintering
◇ ΔT reduction: additional 1–2°C
◇ Combined stack: −10 to −13°C total
